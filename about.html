01 · The short version

Who I am

I'm a junior EE student at the University of Connecticut, working in integrated photonics (Prof. Wang's lab) and semiconductor fabrication (Prof. Gokirmak's lab) at the same time, while TAing Circuits I and keeping way too many side projects alive.

My academic path is aimed at a PhD in EE — probably somewhere at the intersection of photonics and devices. I don't know exactly what the research question is yet, but I'm picking up the skills to find it.

Outside of the lab I care about pedagogy. I think the way introductory circuits is taught leaves students performing procedures they don't understand, and I'm trying to fix that, one practice problem at a time, through circuitspractice.org.

For the record My professor worries I'll be a master of none. I think if I keep moving, I'll be a master of some. The unfinished projects are not a bug — they're proof of range.

03 · Research

Where I work

Prof. Wang · Integrated Photonics

Silicon photonics for AI interconnects

Microring and microdisk resonators for dense wavelength-division multiplexing. I'm implementing Novick's transfer-matrix model in Python, working toward designing full DWDM systems in ANSYS Lumerical to study AI accelerator power bottlenecks. The goal is photonic interconnects that move data between chips without burning the datacenter down.

Prof. Gokirmak · Semiconductor Fabrication

Thin-film characterization & device fab

I built a Python reflectance spectrometer tool that fits Fresnel interference models to OceanOptics HR2000+ spectra and extracts SiO₂ thickness on silicon — measured 90.36 nm to an RMSE of 0.0097. I'm also working on a laser-doped p-n junction and a maskless lithography chamber, both on the lab equipment at UConn's Institute of Materials Science.

The fabrication work and the photonics work end up being the same thing in disguise: both are about controlling light-matter interaction at the nanometer scale, which is a sentence I will be saying for the rest of my career.

06 · Teaching

Why I teach

I think a lot of intro circuits instruction teaches students to execute procedures they don't understand. They can apply KVL correctly and have no idea what a voltage actually is. That bothers me.

My approach: build intuition first, formalism second. That means repeatable practice sets designed around understanding, not rote application, with enough worked examples that a student can trace exactly where their reasoning went wrong.

All of it lives free at circuitspractice.org and on YouTube.
