Class AB Amplifier

MJL3281A / MJL1302A outputs · ~77% efficiency near peak

analogaudioltspice
01 · Overview

Overview

This power amp was built as the ECE 3201 final project in Spring 2026, in collaboration with Karen He. The goal: a portable speaker loud enough for outdoor use (beach, picnic) without breaking the bank — total BOM landed around $40.

The design started from Rod Elliot's P217 schematic, but I hand-measured the Vbe values of my actual transistors and diode forward drops, then tweaked component values until the real-world response matched the sim. It was a fantastic exercise in understanding the gap between a schematic and a working board.

Rod Elliot is genuinely one of the best resources on the web for audio electronics — Elliot Sound Products is worth a bookmark whether or not you're building amps.

02 · How it works

How it works / the build

Since the design follows Rod Elliot's P217 closely, I won't reproduce the full schematic here — it's freely available at the link in the sidebar. Instead, here's the architecture and the tweaks that mattered.

The topology is a standard complementary push-pull Class AB output stage. A 2N3906 PNP input stage feeds a VAS (voltage amplification stage) built around 2N2222 transistors. A diode string (1N4007 × 6) sets the bias voltage across the output stage, with a trimpot (R14) to dial in the quiescent current and minimize crossover distortion without cooking the transistors.

The output transistors are the MJL3281A (NPN) and MJL1302A (PNP) — rated for high current and needed here because the 8 Ω woofer demands ~1.65 A at full power, well beyond what the smaller 2N-series transistors in our parts kit could survive. Both are mounted to a 150×100×18 mm aluminium heatsink to keep junction temps in range.

The speaker is a 4PF-8 4″ surround woofer with a resonant frequency around 137 Hz and a usable range of 100 Hz–10 kHz. For this project that's the right band, though a future version with a dedicated subwoofer (20–200 Hz) and tweeter (2–20 kHz) would fill out the frequency response considerably.

Key circuit values: R6 = 12 kΩ (supply rail), R7 = R8 = 1.8 kΩ (VAS collectors), R9 = 82.5 Ω (bias), R11 = 8 Ω (load), R15 = 20 kΩ (feedback), with 47 µF supply bypass caps and a 4700 µF output capacitor. The input is AC-coupled through a 330 µF cap.

03 · Results

Results & measurements

Peak output8.70 W
Peak efficiency77.3 %
Load8 Ω
Supply30 V
Idle dissipation0.90 W
Max dissipation4.49 W

Efficiency climbs steeply with output power, which is exactly the Class AB behaviour you want — low dissipation at idle, high efficiency near clip. The theoretical maximum for a Class AB stage driving a resistive load from a dual rail is around 78 %; we hit 77.3 %, so the bias tuning worked out well.

04 · Notes

Challenges & what's next

The trickiest part was setting the quiescent bias. Too low and you hear crossover distortion; too high and the output stage runs hot and wastes power at idle. I iterated on the diode string count and the trimpot setting while watching idle dissipation on the bench supply until I found a stable point.

Hand-measuring Vbe for each transistor was slower than just trusting the datasheet typical, but it paid off — the simulated and measured waveforms matched much more closely than they did with nominal values.

If I built this again from scratch (and I plan to), I'd start with a proper PCB instead of breadboard/protoboard, add a soft-start relay to prevent the speaker pop on power-up, and add a crossover network to pair the woofer with a tweeter. A three-way enclosure with proper port tuning would push the bass extension noticeably lower than the 137 Hz woofer resonance allows in free air.
